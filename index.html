Indian Child Welfare Act

Walking With ICWA

Learn the Indian Child Welfare Act through conversation, real scenarios, and hands-on exploration. This is for everyone: attorneys, social workers, Tribal members, families, and anyone wanting to understand ICWA.

A note before you begin ICWA is a federal law, not a racial classification. It recognizes the political relationship between Tribes and the federal government, and the right of Tribes to protect their children and communities.

Active Efforts
Going beyond a checklist
The Hunting Analogy
Think of Active Efforts like preparing for a hunting trip. You do not just hand someone a list of what they need and send them out the door. You make sure they actually have everything, and that it works.

If you are serious about the hunt, you do not show up without your supplies. And if someone in your group is missing theirs, you do not just say "the store exists." You help them get ready.

Active Efforts means the department shows up fully equipped, and helps the family get there too.
Make Sure You Have Your Supplies
The Plan (Your Rifle)
A culturally appropriate, individualized service plan, not a generic template. Know what you are aiming for and why.
The Case (Protecting the Tool)
Documentation of every active effort made. Without a record, the effort may as well not exist in court. Protect your work, write it down.
Know the Territory
Understand the Tribe's resources, culture, and family structure. You cannot help a family navigate terrain you have never looked at.
A Hunting Partner (The Tribe)
Contact the Tribe early and keep them involved. They know the land. They know the family. Do not go out alone when you do not have to.
Timing
Active Efforts must begin before removal, not after. Waiting until a child is already placed to start trying misses the whole point.
